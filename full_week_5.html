Week 5: February fifth to eleventh

Plain Language Summary

This week I tried to notice the things that were beautiful. I found that moving slowly helps with this. But I also noticed that because I move slowly this kind of noticing seems to put me even more at odds with the expectations of the world.

Full text for week five

How can I tell you about the beauty I have noticed this week because of my slow wanderings to and from the bus? How can I write about how slow wandering tunes me into my breath, my heart, my life, reasserting bodily awareness? This slowness that moves me into and through that which is difficult. How can I tell you of these things without you walking away thinking that it makes all the other stuff ok?

You will choose.

To move slowly is as much a way of being in the world as any other, it is one I enjoy. It is also radical in a world that values busy, in a world that wants rush, in a world that pretends I can be a superhero. This week, I had the privilege of reveling in moving ponderously. In my sore fatigue, it was necessary and tiring, but in my sore fatigue it was also welcome.

Slowness this week meant noticing the very granules of snow that clings to last yearâ€™s lilac seeds. Because I must take my time, I notice more readily the way that light glistens off ice perched on plants across the landscape. Slow means noticing how the visual metaphor of a glacier can be found in a 1-foot square. Slow means noticing the beauty of a tree through a campus door window while everyone else rushes by. On Monday I look, for about 3 minutes, on this tree out the window between the ICT tower and the Engineering block at the University of Calgary; it is stunning.

My pace of living is out of sync with the world; how much I wish I could gently hold a moment with each frazzled soul that passes and simply say, you are enough just by being. I would tell them to stop worrying about becoming someone because you already are someone. I would critique busy culture, hustle culture, aspirational culture, but this would be unfair. It is a black hole whose gravitational pull is impossible to avoid. With enough healing, I too will be pulled into its gravitational well. So, I think critique of the rush is fruitless, instead I grieve. Water is a barrier in my movement through space and it is spectacular. What would shift in our hearts if we had the time to breathe in and revel both truths? How many of us would be just as content doing the same work, but slower? How few of us have that choice? Speed is an abled privilege. The hustle keeps me out of full participation.
